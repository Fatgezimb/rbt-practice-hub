Measurement

Measurement turns session events into useful data. Choose the system that matches the behavior, record carefully, and update graphs so the supervisor can review patterns.

Task 1

Continuous Measurement

Use continuous measurement when the plan calls for every occurrence or exact timing value.

Remember

  • Frequency counts responses.
  • Duration times how long behavior lasts.
  • Latency times from cue to response.
  • IRT times between responses.

Example

Counting 12 hand raises during group is frequency. Timing from "clean up" to the first toy placed in the bin is latency.

Watch Out

Checking only at the end of each minute is not continuous measurement because it samples moments.

Quick check: If every response or exact time value is captured, think continuous measurement.
Task 2

Discontinuous Measurement

Use interval or time-sampling systems when the plan calls for a sampled estimate.

Remember

  • Whole interval requires behavior for the full interval.
  • Partial interval records any occurrence in the interval.
  • Momentary time sampling checks only at the exact moment.
  • Permanent product counts an outcome left behind.

Example

Recording whether the learner is seated when a timer beeps is momentary time sampling.

Watch Out

Partial interval may overestimate behavior. Whole interval may underestimate behavior.

Quick check: If the observer checks intervals or moments, it is discontinuous measurement.
Task 3

Data and Graphs

Enter data accurately and update the graph type that matches the data.

Remember

  • Line graphs show repeated data over time.
  • Bar graphs compare categories or totals.
  • Visual analysis looks at level, trend, variability, and changes.

Example

Adding today's number of independent requests to a line graph helps the team see whether requests are increasing.

Watch Out

Do not change treatment based on one data point unless directed by the supervisor.

Quick check: Graphs display data; they do not replace clinical decision-making.

Assessment

Assessment tasks involve collecting information about preferences and behavior patterns. Follow the procedure, record what happens, and avoid turning observations into unsupported conclusions.

Task 4

Preference Assessments

Arrange materials and record selections or engagement according to the assessment format.

Remember

  • Indirect means reported information.
  • Free operant records engagement with available items.
  • Paired choice presents two items.
  • MSWO removes chosen items after selection.

Example

Showing crackers and a ball together and recording crackers as selected is paired choice.

Watch Out

Preferred items are not automatically reinforcers. Reinforcement is shown by increased future behavior.

Quick check: Preference tells what may work; reinforcement is confirmed by behavior change.
Task 5

ABC Data

Separate the antecedent, behavior, and consequence using observable language.

Remember

  • Antecedent happens before behavior.
  • Behavior is what the learner did.
  • Consequence is what followed.
  • Use objective descriptions.

Example

Antecedent: worksheet presented. Behavior: learner pushed paper to floor. Consequence: staff removed paper for 30 seconds.

Watch Out

"Wanted escape" is not an antecedent. It is an interpretation.

Quick check: Another observer should be able to picture the same event from your note.

Skill Acquisition and Behavior Reduction

These tasks cover teaching procedures, prompts, token systems, safety procedures, and behavior-reduction options. Three of tasks 6-14 must be demonstrated with a client.

Task 6

Discrete-Trial Teaching

Run clear SD, response, and consequence trials.

Remember

  • SD signals the opportunity.
  • Response is what the learner does.
  • Consequence follows the response.

Example

"Touch car" followed by a touch and planned praise/token is a clean DTT trial.

Watch Out

Error correction should follow the written plan, not staff frustration.

Quick check: DTT should have one clear instruction, one response chance, and one planned consequence.
Task 7

Naturalistic Teaching

Use motivation and natural activities while still teaching a planned target.

Remember

  • Capture opportunities that already happen.
  • Contrive opportunities by arranging the environment.
  • Use natural reinforcement when possible.

Example

A learner reaches for bubbles, says "bubbles," and gets bubbles.

Watch Out

Naturalistic does not mean unplanned or untracked.

Quick check: The target is planned, but the opportunity feels connected to the activity.
Task 8

Chaining

Teach linked steps in a task analysis.

Remember

  • Forward chaining starts at step one.
  • Backward chaining teaches the last step first.
  • Total task supports all steps each time.

Example

Prompting each handwashing step every trial is total task chaining.

Watch Out

"Get ready" is too broad unless broken into clear steps.

Quick check: Chaining fits skills where one step sets up the next.
Task 9

Shaping

Reinforce closer approximations toward a final behavior.

Remember

  • Terminal behavior is the final goal.
  • Approximations move closer over time.
  • Criteria should change gradually.

Example

Reinforce a sound, then a closer sound, then the full word.

Watch Out

A criterion jump that is too large can stop progress.

Quick check: Ask what closer version will contact reinforcement next.
Task 10

Discrimination Training

Teach when a response should and should not occur.

Remember

  • SD signals reinforcement is available.
  • S-delta signals it is not available.
  • Stimulus control means responding under the right cue.

Example

The learner says "red" for the red card, not the blue card.

Watch Out

If the learner only responds when you point, the prompt may still control the response.

Quick check: The relevant stimulus changes, and the response changes with it.
Task 11

Prompting

Use temporary help and fade it toward independence.

Remember

  • Physical prompts involve touch.
  • Model prompts show the response.
  • Gesture prompts point or motion.
  • Stimulus fading changes material features.

Example

Pointing to the correct picture is a gesture prompt.

Watch Out

Prompts that never fade can create prompt dependence.

Quick check: Prompting helps now; fading plans for independence.
Task 12

Token Systems

Deliver and exchange tokens according to the written plan.

Remember

  • Tokens are conditioned reinforcers.
  • Backup reinforcers are exchanged for tokens.
  • The earning and exchange rules should be predictable.

Example

Five stars can be traded for two minutes of a game when the board is full.

Watch Out

Changing prices mid-session can weaken the system.

Quick check: Tokens work best when earning, saving, and exchange are clear.
Task 13

Crisis / Emergency Procedures

Follow approved protocols within role and training limits.

Remember

  • Prevention happens before escalation.
  • De-escalation responds to early signs.
  • Crisis protocol is followed when safety procedures are required.

Example

Moving extra materials away before a known hard transition can be prevention.

Watch Out

Do not invent safety procedures. Follow the approved plan.

Quick check: In crisis work, the plan and training decide the response.
Task 14

Behavior Reduction Option

Demonstrate antecedent intervention, differential reinforcement, or extinction.

Remember

  • Antecedent interventions happen before behavior.
  • Differential reinforcement strengthens selected behavior patterns.
  • Extinction withholds the maintaining reinforcer.

Example

Reinforcing hand raising instead of calling out is DRA.

Watch Out

Ignoring is not extinction unless attention is the maintaining reinforcer and safety is addressed.

Quick check: Are we changing what happens before, what gets reinforced, or what no longer gets reinforced?

Professionalism and Requirements

These tasks focus on objective documentation, dignity, boundaries, supervision, and knowing when to seek clinical direction.

Task 15

Session Notes

Write objective notes about what happened in session.

Remember

  • Use observable language.
  • Include relevant data and events.
  • Avoid opinions and labels.

Example

"Learner completed 8 of 10 matching trials independently."

Watch Out

"Learner was lazy" is subjective and disrespectful.

Quick check: If the note includes a judgment, replace it with what happened.
Task 16

Client Dignity

Protect privacy, choice, respect, and age-appropriate communication.

Remember

  • Use respectful tone.
  • Protect privacy.
  • Offer choices when available.
  • Speak about clients respectfully.

Example

Discussing behavior privately instead of in a hallway protects dignity.

Watch Out

Baby talk with an adolescent is not age-appropriate.

Quick check: Would this still feel respectful if the client or family heard it?
Task 17

Professional Boundaries

Keep communication and relationships within the therapy role.

Remember

  • Use professional communication.
  • Avoid dual relationships.
  • Handle gifts through policy and supervision.
  • Redirect irrelevant personal conversations.

Example

Decline a personal gift respectfully and notify the supervisor.

Watch Out

Adding a caregiver on personal social media creates boundary risk.

Quick check: If the relationship extends outside therapy, pause and ask.
Task 18

Supervision Requirements

Know core supervision expectations and verify current standards.

Remember

  • At least 5 percent of service hours are supervised each month.
  • At least two face-to-face live meetings occur monthly.
  • At least one monthly observation occurs while services are provided.

Example

If you provide 80 service hours, 5 percent is 4 supervised hours.

Watch Out

Casual messages alone do not replace required live supervision contacts.

Quick check: Know the numbers, but verify official requirements.
Task 19

Clinical Direction

Seek supervisor guidance before changing procedures or handling unclear situations independently.

Remember

  • Ask about new or unclear goals.
  • Ask about challenging behavior or safety concerns.
  • Refer parent requests outside the plan.
  • Pause for ethical uncertainty.

Example

A parent asks you to add toilet training. You acknowledge the request and tell the supervisor.

Watch Out

Changing reinforcement schedules on your own is outside the RBT role.

Quick check: If the next step changes the plan, affects safety, or raises ethics concerns, seek direction.
